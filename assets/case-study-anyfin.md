# Anyfin — Product Strategy & Savings Rebuild

**Role:** Business Data Analyst → Business Product Manager
**Period:** August 2021 – September 2022
**Location:** Stockholm, Sweden

---

## Context

Anyfin is a fintech company that helps consumers refinance high-interest loans at better rates. Beyond refinancing, the app offered a savings and budgeting product designed to help users build financial health over time.

I joined as a Business Data Analyst, owning all app-user engagement analytics. After four months, I moved into the Business Product Manager role and took ownership of the savings and budgeting product — a feature area that was underperforming against its potential.

The challenge was twofold: get the analytics foundation right first, then use it to drive product decisions that actually moved the needle.

---

## Approach

### Phase 1 — Analytics Foundation (Business Data Analyst)

**Standardizing the analytics layer**

When I joined, A/B testing and user event tracking were inconsistent across the product. Different teams were measuring the same actions differently, making experiment results unreliable and product decisions contested.

I owned all app-user engagement analytics in BigQuery and rebuilt the tracking layer — standardizing event schemas, defining the canonical metric set, and creating shared dashboards that became the company's source of truth for user behaviour.

**Identifying product improvements**

With a reliable data foundation, I ran systematic analyses across the user funnel to identify conversion drop-offs and engagement gaps. The output was a prioritized backlog of product improvements, each quantified by potential impact.

Executing against that backlog improved **conversion rates by 26%** across key product flows.

**Expanding to new markets**

I built the SQL models and led market research to support Anyfin's expansion into new geographies — providing the analytical inputs for launch decisions on customer sizing, acquisition economics, and product fit.

### Phase 2 — Product Strategy (Business Product Manager)

**Taking ownership of the savings product**

Moving into the PM role, I inherited a savings and budgeting feature that had low engagement and wasn't delivering on its promise as a financial health tool. User activity was flat, average saved balances were modest, and the product lacked a clear strategic direction.

I defined a new product strategy built around three pillars: reduce friction in the core save-and-track flow, add features that create meaningful engagement reasons, and make financial progress visible and motivating.

**Leading cross-functional delivery**

I led a 13-member cross-functional team — product, design, engineering, and data — through delivery of new savings and budgeting features. This included a rebuilt savings flow, new goal-setting functionality, and an improved dashboard for tracking financial progress.

The work required keeping a large, distributed team aligned on priorities, managing trade-offs between scope and delivery speed, and maintaining stakeholder confidence throughout.

**Outcomes of the product rebuild**

The rebuilt savings product doubled average saved balances per user. The broader product strategy, including the new engagement features, drove a **100% increase in user activity and retention**.

More granularly, the new features contributed a **20% increase in user activity** and a **15% improvement in retention** — metrics tracked weekly and validated against the pre-launch baseline.

---

## Outcomes

- **26% conversion improvement** from analytics-driven product fixes (data analyst phase)
- **100% increase in user activity and retention** from product strategy execution (PM phase)
- **2x average saved balances per user** from savings product rebuild
- **20% user activity increase** and **15% retention improvement** from new features
- Analytics infrastructure rebuilt: standardized A/B testing and event tracking across the company
- Multi-market launch analytics built from scratch

---

## Learnings

**The data-to-PM transition is a leverage multiplier.** Coming into the PM role with six months of deep analytics context meant I already knew where the bodies were buried — which metrics were noisy, which user segments behaved differently, which A/B tests had been run and failed. That context compressed the product discovery cycle significantly.

**Retention is a product problem, not a marketing problem.** Early analysis showed that retention issues at Anyfin weren't about acquisition quality or brand — they were about the product not giving users a strong enough reason to return. Reframing the problem that way changed the entire product roadmap.

**Clarity on the job-to-be-done unlocks team alignment.** The savings product had accumulated features without a shared view of what it was actually trying to help users do. Getting that clarity — and writing it down simply — was what aligned a 13-person team and reduced decision churn throughout delivery.
